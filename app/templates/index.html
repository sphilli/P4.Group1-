How Tornados are Formed

Thunderstorms develop when cold and dry air is pushed over warm and humid air. Since warm air rises and goes through the cold air, that causes a change in wind direction, otherwise known as an updraft. Greatly varying winds from the thunderstorm then cause the updraft to rotate and continuously draw in more warm air from the already-formed thunderstorm. The rotation speed increases and a funnel cloud forms to become a twister. As it gains strength, the funnel becomes longer and becomes the most dangerous when it touches the ground, topping 250 mph. Though tornados usually last between 10 minutes and an hour, the longest recorded tornado lasted 3 1/2 hours and traveled 219 miles through Missouri, Illinois, and Indiana.
